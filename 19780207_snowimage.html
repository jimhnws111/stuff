Blizzard of '78 changed Toms River girl's life

KATHRYN CURTO
CONTRIBUTOR

Editor's Note:Kathryn Curto grew up in Toms River and recalls a momentous event during the Blizzard of '78. Her story centers on a shopping trip with her father to the old A&P on Route 37. That location has since been home to the Victory Diner, and now a Wawa occupies the space.

"Oh, Madonna look at it out there! A friggin' blizzard for God's sake!" my father says pushing open the front door.

"Jesus, Freddy, it's really coming down," my mother says.

I watch them from the end of the hallway. The wind makes the bottom of her blue velour robe sway and rub across her ankles. And after he closes the door, he wipes off the snowflakes that had blown in and landed on her shoulders and her hair. Jim at Joel Richard hair salon had done a beehive last Saturday, and it needed to last her the whole week.

"I need to get to A&P before it gets any worse," my mother says. "I need to pick up some things. Today's a good soup day. I'll make a nice pot of vegetable beef soup."

When will the weather bring us a good milkshake and pizza day? This is what I'd like to know.

We all walk back into the kitchen and my mother pours my father his coffee. I sit down and eat my Quisp and try to figure out the answers to the riddles on the side of the box.

My father puts the milk in his coffee stirring hard and fast. The spoon clinks on the inside of the cup. Some mornings I hear his clinking spoon from my bedroom — that's how I know it's time to get up for school and that he's leaving for work soon. My father likes to get to the gas station early — Toms River Exxon — do his bookwork and get ready for customers.

"Whaddaya nuts?" my father blurts. I smell his cigarette breath. "You can't drive in this weather!"

He zips up his jacket and blows his nose into the flimsy white handkerchief he keeps in his back pocket.

"I'm closing the shop today — nobody's coming out in this weather. Just gimme a list. I'll go. And, you," he says and points to me, "you go get dressed. You're coming with me."

Everything stops. Everything stands still. Frozen. Silent.

My father and I never go anywhere together, just the two of us. It's always my mom and me. Frick and frack, she calls us.

Now what? I have nothing to say to him if other people aren't around. The radio announcers and television guys are going on and on about how this blizzard, the Blizzard of '78, they're calling it, could be the worst one that has hit in decades.

The shiver I feel has nothing to do with the snow. It comes from the place in me where worry grows. I imagine us alone in the car. I imagine the silence. His cigarettes. The sound of my gum cracking.

Swallow and blink. I tell myself. Swallow and blink.

I am 11 and this is the first time, ever, that I see my father in a grocery store.

I heard stories from my mother about when I was a little baby, how he sometimes picked up formula or diaper pins on his way home from work, but I never believed her.

I just thought it was another one of those stories that, on good days, made him out to be someone he really wasn't. Some grocery-shopping Daddy who wore faded blue jeans, listened to rock 'n' roll music and read Dr. Seuss books at bedtime. The father I know wears a work uniform and shines his shoes at night. He listens to Sinatra and Jerry Vale and reads only the Asbury Park Press, and that doesn't happen at bedtime. It happens at dinnertime.

And now here we are. Buying soup stuff together, roaming the aisles at the A&P during the Blizzard of '78.

I'm wheeling the cart up and he's shuffling around looking like he's lost in China or Africa or some other faraway place. But something happens and his weird look fades. His face brightens. He's happy.

The snow is coming down harder and harder, but somehow it's not mattering to him. Not to me, either. We pick through all of the greens and find the freshest they have left. We search the shelves of the meat department for exactly the kind of bones my mother wants for her soup because she says the bones are what give it the flavor. And we buy lots of stuff she didn't even put on the list — root beer, black licorice, lupini beans, butterscotch pudding, hard salami. All the stuff he likes.

Funny how things are. How things turn out sometimes. Root beer, black licorice, lupini beans, butterscotch pudding, hard salami.

This is all the stuff I like, too.

This is an excerpt from Curto's manuscript, "Preparing Ourselves," about growing up at the Jersey Shore during the 1970s and 1980s. Learn more at www.kathycurto.com.

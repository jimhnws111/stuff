THE BLIZZARD OF '93 -- Early Warnings, Then Snow, Leave a Stillness Over 3 States; New Jersey: Malls Don't Open And Buses Stop

By Charles Strum

The snow began falling before dawn today, and it seemed as if it would never stop. It snowed so much that New Jersey's major highways were all but impassable, its mass transit paralyzed and its biggest shopping malls closed.

And along the coastline, the state's much-battered beaches lost more sand to wind and tide, with a high midnight tide threatening to cut off roads and power.

As communities in Monmouth and Ocean counties evacuated their residents to temporary shelters, hundreds of motorists were reported stranded on parts of interstate highways. Extra state troopers were called in to assist them.

Most major roads -- the turnpike, the Garden State Parkway and the interstates -- remained open, though, with at least one passable lane in each direction for emergencies. N.J. Transit Buses Go Home

State and local authorities said secondary roads were undependable, and some counties, among them Mercer, Burlington, Hunterdon and Somerset, closed all major county roads.

In Bergen County, Route 4 and Route 17 were open, but just barely, said Capt. Ed Schmalz of the Bergen County police. Statewide only three well-traveled highways were officially closed: Route 30 into Atlantic City, because of flooding; Route 31 in Hopewell Township in Mercer County, because of snow; and Route 36 (Ocean Avenue) in Sea Bright.

At 1:45 P.M., N.J. Transit, the state-run mass-transit agency, suspended bus service throughout the state and told all drivers to return to their garages. A spokesman, Armando Arrastia, said train service on the Northeast corridor between Trenton, Newark and New York was behind schedule because of switch problems caused by snow or ice.

Jersey Central Power and Light and Public Service Electric and Gas reported about 25,000 customers without power; most were in Monmouth and Ocean Counties on the coast. Both utilities expected that figure to grow as winds increased.

Gov. Jim Florio declared a state of emergency at 10:30 this morning, which permits immediate use of National Guard, state police and other agencies for rescue or repair duty.

A spokesman for the state's emergency management office in West Trenton, John Hagerty, said the state was prepared for up to two feet of snow in the higher elevations of northwestern New Jersey, up to a foot in northern and central areas and 6 to 8 inches along the coast. He said there had been no reports of deaths.

In Sea Bright, Police Chief Ken Johnson said that after high tide at 11:30 A.M., there was three to four feet of water on Ocean Avenue. Power was out and the police ordered everyone in town evacuated to emergency shelters.

In Monmouth Beach, Mayor Lou Sodano said the early tide had not flooded the town, but that "as soon as there is a water problem, or the lights go out, the shelter will open up."

In northern New Jersey, the Willowbrook Mall in Wayne and several major Bergen County shopping centers closed or did not open.

Toms River Climate Information

One or more layers of stratocumulus, not a result of spreading cumulus

Stratocumulus (like the continuous area seen in the image above) often forms when moisture is trapped beneath what is known as a subsidence inversion. Warmer and drier air just above the cloud layer acts like a ceiling, forcing the moisture to spread out along and under the ceiling. Typically, skies are mainly clear above the cloud layer, but few breaks in the clouds are noted, as is the case in this image. If the warmer and drier air remains in place above the cloud layer, the stratocumulus deck can stay in place for days.

Since the moisture feeding the clouds is relatively shallow, little or no precipitation is expected. The persistent stratocumulus deck in this image was captured over Boulder CO, which was remarkably lush and green at the time.
